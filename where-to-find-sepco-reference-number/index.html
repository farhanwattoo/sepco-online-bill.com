How to Find Your SEPCO Reference Number (5 Methods)

The 14-digit reference number is printed on every SEPCO bill — but if no bill survives, you cannot simply look it up online by name or CNIC. This guide walks through five practical ways to recover the number, from a thirty-second search of your phone gallery to a visit to the sub-division office, and shows how to keep it safe so you never lose it again.

Here is the short answer. Your SEPCO reference number sits in the top section of every paper bill, near your name and address — so any old bill, or any photo of an old bill, gives it to you instantly. If nothing survives, a neighbour's bill reveals most of the digits your street shares, and your local SEPCO sub-division office can find the account in its own records using your CNIC and address. The 118 helpline can point you to the right office if you are not sure which one serves your area.

What does not work is any form of online self-service recovery: the bill portal has no search by name, CNIC, or meter number, and no official app will reveal the number from your identity alone. The five methods below are ordered from fastest to slowest, so start at the top and stop as soon as one works. Once you have the number, the bill check guide covers the lookup itself step by step.

Method 1: Any Old Paper Bill

The reference number never changes — it is tied to the connection, not to a month — so a bill from last year is exactly as good as this month's. Search the usual places: the drawer or file where receipts go, the plastic bag of paid bills many households keep, behind the fridge magnet, inside the electricity meter box itself where meter readers sometimes tuck bills.

When you find one, look at the top portion of the page. The reference number is printed in a boxed row near your name and address, usually the most prominent number on the bill, often with the digits grouped into small boxes or separated by gaps. Ignore the grouping — the number is the full run of 14 digits read left to right. Do not confuse it with the meter serial (printed lower down, sometimes with letters) or the 10-digit customer ID. The format guide breaks down what each group of digits means if you want to double-check you have the right one.

Method 2: A Photo or Scan on Your Phone

Even when every paper bill is gone, a picture of one often survives. People photograph bills constantly — to send to a spouse, to a landlord, to a payment agent — and then forget the photo exists. Spend two minutes searching:

  • Phone gallery: scroll to months when you remember paying, or use your gallery's search for "document" or "text" if it supports it.
  • WhatsApp: open chats with family members, your landlord, or the shop where you pay, and check the shared-media view. Bill photos pile up there.
  • Email and cloud backups: if you ever scanned a bill for an office or a bank, search attachments for "bill" or "SEPCO".

Zoom in on the top section of the photographed bill and copy the 14 digits carefully — a blurry photo is the classic source of one-wrong-digit errors. If the portal rejects the number, re-read the photo before assuming the number is wrong.

Method 3: The Neighbour's-Bill Trick

Reference numbers are not random. The 14 digits encode the batch your area belongs to, your sub-division office, and your individual account. Because everyone on the same street is billed through the same batch and sub-division, a neighbour's bill shows you most of your own number — everything except the account portion that is unique to your meter.

  1. Ask a next-door neighbour to show you any recent bill (or a photo of one).
  2. Note the batch and sub-division digits from the start of their reference number — yours will match or sit very close.
  3. Take those digits, plus your CNIC and address, to the sub-division office and ask the staff to confirm your full account number against their records.

This trick does not give you the complete number by itself — never guess the remaining digits, because a valid-looking guess can pull up someone else's bill. Its real value is that it tells the office staff exactly which batch register to open, which turns a slow search into a quick confirmation. The reference number guide explains the batch and sub-division structure in more depth.

Method 4: Your Local SEPCO Sub-Division Office

Every SEPCO connection belongs to a sub-division office, and that office holds the consumer records for its area. This is the method that works even when nothing else does, because the staff can look up your account by name and address in their own records — something the online portal cannot do.

Bring your original CNIC and know the exact address of the connection: street, house identification, and nearby landmarks all help. If the connection is registered under a parent's or previous owner's name, mention that name too, since the record will be filed under it. The staff will trace the account and give you the reference number; while you are there, ask them to print a duplicate bill so you leave with the number on paper.

Tip: go earlier in the month if you can. Offices are busiest in the days around the due date, when payment queues are longest.

Method 5: The 118 Helpline

If you do not know which sub-division office serves your area — common after moving house or in newly split localities — call the 118 electricity helpline. The helpline will not read your reference number out over the phone, but it can tell you which office holds your record and what to bring. That guidance saves a wasted trip to the wrong office, which in rural districts can mean a long journey. Treat 118 as the navigation step before Method 4, not as a lookup service in itself.

Keep It Safe Once You Have It

You only ever need to recover the number once — if you store it properly. Because it stays the same for the life of the connection, one minute of effort now saves the whole hunt next time:

  • Save it as a phone contact — create a contact called "SEPCO Bill" and put the 14 digits in the number or notes field. Contacts sync to your account, so it survives a lost phone.
  • Add it to your notes app, labelled with the address it belongs to if you handle bills for more than one house.
  • Photograph the current bill and favourite the photo so it does not get lost in the gallery.
  • Write it on the meter box in permanent marker — a habit many households in upper Sindh already follow, and the reason a meter box is worth checking in Method 1.

Keep it private within reason. Anyone with the number can view the bill, which shows the account holder's name and address. Sharing it with family or a payment agent is normal; posting it publicly is not.

With the number recovered and saved, the next step is the lookup itself — see SEPCO bill check by reference number for the exact portal steps, common errors, and how to save a duplicate copy.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I find my SEPCO reference number online with my CNIC or name?

No. The bill portal accepts only the 14-digit reference number — there is no online lookup by CNIC, name, or meter number. Recovering the number always goes through an old bill, a photo of one, a neighbour's bill plus an office visit, the sub-division office itself, or guidance from the 118 helpline.

Is the reference number the same as the meter number or customer ID?

No. The meter serial is stamped on the meter itself and is not accepted by the bill portal. The customer ID is a separate 10-digit identifier that works on some payment channels. Only the 14-digit reference number works for the standard online bill check.

Does the reference number change if the bill is in a previous owner's name?

No. The reference number belongs to the connection, not the person, so a bill printed in a previous owner's or landlord's name still carries the correct number for that meter and works for checking the current bill.

What should I take to the SEPCO sub-division office?

Take your original CNIC and know the exact address of the connection, including street and nearby landmarks. If the connection is registered in another family member's name, knowing that name speeds the search. Any surviving bill fragment or a neighbour's bill also helps the staff narrow down the record.

Can a bank agent or shopkeeper find my reference number for me?

Bank and payment agents can pull up a bill once you give them the reference number, but they cannot search by your name or CNIC any more than you can. If you have no number at all, the sub-division office or a neighbour's bill is the route that works.

Which method is fastest for recovering the reference number?

Checking your phone's photo gallery and WhatsApp for a picture of an old bill is usually fastest — many people have photographed a bill at some point without remembering it. A paper bill anywhere in the house is equally instant. The office visit is the slowest but works even when nothing else does.
